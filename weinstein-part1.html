From Symplectic Manifolds to Hilbert Spaces: A Philosophical Analysis of Weinstein’s Geometric Quantization Framework

2025-07-06 by Gustav

INTRODUCTION

Geometric quantization seeks to provide a mathematically rigorous and conceptually transparent method for constructing quantum theories from classical phase spaces. Rooted in the language of symplectic geometry, the approach identifies classical systems with symplectic manifolds and attempts to systematically associate to them Hilbert spaces of quantum states, together with operators corresponding to observables. Alan Weinstein's formulation — building on and extending earlier work by Kostant and Souriau — profoundly enriches this framework by emphasizing the role of symplectic groupoids, Lie algebroids, and Poisson geometry in the quantization process.

This essay explores the philosophical foundations and implications of Weinstein’s geometric quantization scheme. Specifically, it investigates how mathematical structures such as prequantum line bundles, polarizations, and the integration of Lie algebroids shape our understanding of the classical-to-quantum transition. Beyond the technical apparatus, the scheme raises fundamental questions: What constitutes a quantum system? Is quantization a functorial process, or must we abandon categorical coherence at the quantum threshold? Does the geometric machinery reflect an ontological continuity between classical and quantum domains, or merely a heuristic bridge?

In analyzing Weinstein’s contributions, this essay situates geometric quantization within broader philosophical debates on intertheoretic reduction, structural realism, and the epistemology of mathematical representation in physics. The aim is not only to clarify the internal logic of Weinstein’s approach but also to assess its adequacy in capturing the ontological and epistemic shifts demanded by quantum theory.

A PRIMER ON SYMPLETIC GEOMETRY

Let \(M\) be any topological space. We define a chart on \(M\) as the pair \((U, \phi)\), where \(U \subset M\) is open and \(\phi: U \rightarrow M\) is such that \(\phi(U) \subset \mathbb{R}^n\) is also open. Given a collection \(\{U_i, \phi_i\}\) of those charts, we say that it is an atlas \(\mathcal{A}\) on \(M\) if they are a cover for \(M\), i.e., if \[\bigcup_{i \in [1,n]} U_i = M\] and if all of it's charts are compatible with each other, i.e., if for any \(i, j \in \mathbb{N} \) such that \(U_i \cap U_j \neq \emptyset\), we have \(\phi_{i,j}: U_{i,j} \rightarrow \phi_{i,j}(U_{i,j}) \in C^\infty(M)\), \(\phi_i \circ \phi_j^{-1}: \phi_i (U_i \cap U_j) \rightarrow \phi_j (\phi_i \cap \phi_j) \subset \mathbb{R}^n\) and \(\phi_j \circ \phi_i^{-1}:\phi_j (U_i \cap U_j) \rightarrow \phi_i (U_i \cap U_j)\subset \mathbb{R}^n \), given \( \phi_{i,j} (U_i \cap U_j)\) open subsets of \(\mathbb{R}^n\). We say that an atlas \(\mathcal{A}\) is maximal for \(M\) if, for any other atlas \(\mathcal{A}'\), we have that if \(\mathcal{A} \cup \mathcal{A}'\) is an atlas for \(M\), then \(\mathcal{A}' \subseteq \mathcal{A}\). Now, suppose that \(\text{dim}(M)=n\). The map \(\phi_i = (x^1, \dots, x^n)\) is smooth on \(M\) and define the local chart \((x^1, \dots, x^n)\) on \(U_i\). Furthermore, anyone with a background in undergraduate level physics or linear algebra had, at some point, to change from one local chart to another (from, e.g., cylindrical to polar); to do so in the context of manifolds, we use the composite of two maps that compose two distinct charts, i.e. \[ \phi_i \circ \phi_j^{-1} (x) = (y^1(x^1,\dots,x^n), \dots, y^n (x^1,\dots, x^n)) \] Finally, \(M\), together with it's maximal atlas, is what we define as a smooth manifold. A function \(f: M \rightarrow \mathbb{R} \) is said to be smooth if, for every \(p \in M\) and for every \(i \in [1,n]\), there is a chart \((U_i, \phi_i)\) with \(x \in U_i\) such that \(f_i \doteq f \circ \phi^{-1}_i: \phi_i (U_i) \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) is a smooth function on the open set \(\phi_i (U_i)\). Other than that, we can represent a smooth function in local coordinates via the function \(\hat{f}: \phi(U) \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^n\), given by \(\hat{f}(p) = f \circ \phi^{-1} (p)\). Then, we can generalize this notion of smooth functions to smooth maps between manifolds. Let \(M\) and \(N\) be smooth manifolds. We say that the map \(F: M \rightarrow N\) is smooth if, for every \(p \in M\), there exist smooth charts \((U,\phi)\), containing \(p\) and \((V, \psi)\) containing \(F(p)\) such that \(F(U) \subseteq V\) and the composite map \(\psi \circ F \circ \phi^{-1}\) is smooth from \(\phi(U)\) to \(\psi(V)\). A primer example of a smooth maps between smooth manifolds are diffeomorphisms, that can be used to characterize sameness on a dimensional level of two smooth manifolds, via the following theorem:

Diffeomorphism Invariance of Dimension

A nonempty smooth manifold of dimension \(m\) cannot be diffeomorphic to an \(n\)-dimensional smooth manifold unless \(m = n\).

and on a boundary level via the theorem:

Diffeomorphism Invariance of Dimension

Suppose \(M\) and \(N\) are smooth manifolds with boundary and \(F:M \rightarrow N\) is a Diffeomorphism. Then, \(F(\partial M) = \partial N\) and \(F\) restricts to a Diffeomorphism from \(\text{Int} M \) to \(\text{Int} N\).

Being honest with you, we will not use any of these results in what follows, but I found it interesting to state them. Given \( f \in C^\infty (M)\) and \( \{ r^i \}_{i \in [0,n]} \) Darboux coordinates on an smooth manifold \(M\), such that \(x^i (t) = (r^i \circ \phi)(t) \). Then, a partial derivative of \(f\) at a point \(p \in M\) is defined as \[ \left.\frac{\partial }{\partial x^i} \right\vert_{p} f \doteq \frac{\partial f}{\partial x^i}(p) \doteq \frac{\partial}{\partial r^i} (f \circ \phi^{-1}) (\phi(p)) \doteq \left. \frac{\partial }{\partial r^i} \right\vert_{\phi(p)} (f \circ \phi^{-1}) \] As the pullback of the partial derivative of \(f\) is smooth, i.e., \[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x^i} \circ \phi^{-1} \in C^\infty (\phi(U)), \] one can cleary infer that \(\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x^i} \) is smooth on \(M\). Next, we a have proposition that is very often used in physics, mainly in general relativity:

Proposition 0.1

Consider \((U, x)\), with \(x = x^1, \dots, x^n\), a chart on a smooth manifold \(M\). Then, \(\dfrac{\partial x^i}{\partial x^j} = \delta^{i}_{j}\)

Consider \(p \in M\), then

\[ \frac{\partial x^i}{\partial x^j} (p) = \frac{\partial}{\partial r^j} (x^i \circ \phi^{-1}) (\phi(p)) = \frac{\partial r^i}{\partial r^j} (\phi(p)) = \delta^{i}_{j} \]
□
And a definition that we'll use in what follows:

Theorem 0.1

Consider \(M\) and \(N\) smooth manifolds and \((U, \phi)\) and \((V, \psi)\) charts on them. Let \(F: N \rightarrow M\) be a smooth map such that \(F(U) \subset V\). Then, we denote the i-th coordinate of \(F\) on the chart \((V, \psi)\) by

\[ F^i \doteq y^i \circ F \doteq r^i \circ \psi \circ F:U \rightarrow \mathbb{R}. \]

We then define the matrix \(\left[\dfrac{\partial F^i}{\partial x^j}\right]\) as the Jacobian matrix of \(F\) relative to the charts \((U, \phi)\) and \((V, \psi)\).

So, as state above, with that in hand, we can enunciate the following thereom:

Inverse Function Theorem

Let \(F : W \to \mathbb{R}^n\) be a \(C^\infty (\mathbb{R}^n)\) map defined on an open subset \( W \) of \(\mathbb{R}^n \) For any point \(p \in W \), the map F is locally invertible at \(p \)if and only if the Jacobian determinant \(\det\left[\dfrac{\partial F^i}{\partial x^j}(p)\right] \) is not zero.

Usually seem in the context of introductory analysis courses. We then define a tensor of rank \((p, q)\) as an algebraic object defined as a multilinear map

\[T: \underbrace{V^* \times \dots \times V^*}_{p \; \text{times}} \times \underbrace{V \times \dots \times V}_{q \; \text{times}} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\]

given \(V\) a vector space \(-\) for instance, the soon to be defined \(T_pM\) and his big brother \(TM\) are vector spaces \(-\) and \(V^*\) it's dual, i.e. the space of linear maps \(\xi: V \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) — which is himself a vector space. A differential form is then defined as an special type of tensor, in the sense that a differential \(p\)-form \(\omega\) is a completely antisymmetric \((0,p)\)-tensor, i.e., a tensor that for any \(i\) and \(j\) satisfies \(\omega (x^1,\dots, x^i, \dots, x^j, \dots, x^p) = - \omega (x^1,\dots, x^j, \dots, x^i, \dots, x^p) \). The space of these forms is often denoted by \(\Omega^p(M)\). A differential form of particular interest is the sympletic form. Simply put, we define a \(2-\)form as non degenerate if \(\forall u (\omega (v,u) = 0 \Rightarrow v = 0)\); but, a more substancial and useful criteria to do so, in the context of vector spaces, is found in the following proposition:

Proposition 0.2

Let \(V\) be a finite dimensional vector space and \(\omega\) a \(2-\)form on \(V\). Then, we say that \(\omega\) is non degenerate if, and only if, \(dimV = 2n\), given \(n \in \mathbb{N}\), and \(\omega^n = \omega \wedge \dots \wedge \omega\) is a volume on \(V\), i.e., if \(\omega \neq 0\).

But one can easlisy generalize this to smooth manifolds: a \(2-\)form \(\omega\) on \(M\) is non degenerate if, and only if, \(dim M = 2n\), given \(n \in \mathbb{N}\), and if \(\omega^n = \omega \wedge \dots \wedge \omega\) defines a volume form on \(M\). Given a crescent interval \(I = (i_1,\dots,i_p)\), with \(1 \leq i_1 \leq \dots \leq i_p \leq n\), the exterior derivative \(d: \Omega^p(M) \to \Omega^{p+1}(M)\) of \(\omega \in \Omega^{p}(M)\) is given, in local coordinates, by

\[ d \omega = \sum_{I} \left(\sum_{i \in [1,n]} \frac{\partial \omega_{I}}{\partial x^j} dx^j \right) \wedge dx^I \]

If \(d \omega = 0\), we say that \(\omega\) is closed. Moreover, if \(\omega\) is both closed and non degenerate, then \(\omega\) is called sympletic, and the pair \((M, \omega\)) is defined as a sympletic manifold . Thereafter, consider \(p \in M\). A linear map \(\gamma: C^\infty(M) \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) is called a derivation at \(p\) if it satisfies \(\gamma(fg) = f(p)\gamma g + g(p)\gamma f\), for all \(f,g \in C^\infty(M)\). The set of all smooth derivations at \(p\), denoted by \(T_p M \), is what we define as the tangent space to \(M\) on \(p\). The tangent bundle of \(M\) is then defined as

\[ TM \doteq \bigsqcup_{p \in M} T_p M \]

The dual of the tangent bundle, in the sense we defined above, is the cotangent space \((T_pM)^*\) and, similarly, the bundle \((TM)^*\) is the disjoint union of all of these spaces. The canonical basis for \((TM)^*\) is given with respect to the canonical basis of \(M\), as \(\{\partial/\partial x^i \} =(\partial/\partial x^1, \dots, \partial/\partial x^n)\). Now, consider two smooth manifolds \(M\) and \(N\) with \(\text{dim}(M, N) = n\) and a map \(F: M \rightarrow N\). We then define the map

\[ dF_p: T_pM \rightarrow T_{F(p)}N \]

as the differential of \(F\) at \(p\). This means that, given \(\gamma \in T_p M\) and \(f \in C^\infty(M)\), we have that \(dF_p(\gamma)(f) = \gamma (f \circ F)\). Noting that

\[ \begin{split} dF_p(\gamma)(fg) = \gamma ((fg) \circ F) &= \gamma((f \circ F)(g \circ F)) \\ &= f \circ F(p) \gamma (g \circ F) + g \circ F(p) \gamma (f \circ F) \\ &= f(F(p))dF_p(\gamma)(g) + g(F(p))dF_p (\gamma)(f) \end{split} \]

one can conclude that \(dF_p (\gamma): C^\infty(N) \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) is a derivation at \(F(p)\). The differential have the following properties:

  1. Linearity:
    For all \( v, w \in T_p M \) and \( \lambda \in \mathbb{R} \), \[ dF_p(\lambda v + w) = \lambda dF_p(v) + dF_p(w). \]
  2. Chain Rule:
    If \( G: N \to P \) is another smooth map, then: \[ d(G \circ F)_p = dG_{F(p)} \circ dF_p. \]
  3. Differential of the Identity:
    If \( F = \text{Id}_M \), then \( dF_p = \text{Id}_{T_p M} \).
  4. Local Coordinate Expression:
    In charts \( (U, \phi) \) around \( p \) and \( (V, \psi) \) around \( F(p) \), the differential is represented by the Jacobian matrix of \( \psi \circ F \circ \phi^{-1} \): \[ dF_p \left( \frac{\partial}{\partial x^i} \bigg|_p \right) = \sum_{j=1}^n \frac{\partial (\psi^j \circ F \circ \phi^{-1})}{\partial x^i} \bigg|_{\phi(p)} \frac{\partial}{\partial y^j} \bigg|_{F(p)}. \]
  5. Functoriality:
    The assignment \( F \mapsto dF \) respects composition and identity, making \( d(-) \) a covariant functor from the category of smooth manifolds to vector bundles.
  6. Differential and Submanifolds:
    If \( F \) is an immersion/submersion/embedding, then \( dF_p \) is injective/surjective/bijective.
  7. Relation to Cotangent Spaces:
    The dual map (pullback) \( F^* : T^*_{F(p)} N \to T^*_p M \) acts on covectors.

THE BASICS ON QUANTUM AND CLASSICAL MECHANICS

It is natural that a rigorous discussion on geometric quantization and its intricacies requires an introduction to both classical and quantum mechanics, along with an ontologically and mathematically precise delineation of the physical systems each theory describes. Therefore, in this chapter, I undertake this task. In what follows, we will establish the formal definitions of these systems, highlighting their underlying mathematical structures and the ontological principles that distinguish them, with the aim of providing greater clarity between the formalism presented here and canonical systems in the literature through elementary examples. With this in mind, we begin our chapter by characterizing classical systems:

Definition

Consider a symplectic manifold \((M, \omega)\) equipped with a smooth function \(\text{H} \in C^{\infty}(M)\). The vector field \(X_H\), determined by the expression

\[ d\text{H} = - \iota_{X_H} \omega, \]

is called the Hamiltonian vector field with Hamiltonian (or energy function) \(\text{H}\). We then define the triple \((M, \omega, X_H)\) as a classical physical system.

Qualitatively, we assign to the underlying space \(M\) the role of the system's phase space, while the pair \((M, \omega)\) encapsulates its kinematics, as it provides the geometric structure defining how physical states evolve, and the function \(\text{H}\) its dynamics, as it describes how its physical states evolve in time. Below, through examples, I establish a connection between canonical classical systems in the literature and the formalism presented:

Example

Consider a one-dimensional harmonic oscillator. In this case, we have \(M = T^*\mathbb{R}\), \(\omega = dp \wedge dq\), and \(H(p(t),q(t)) = \dfrac{1}{2}(p^2(t) + q^2(t))\), where \(\{p(t), q(t)\}\) is a Darboux coordinate system on \(T^*\mathbb{R}\).

Example

Consider a spinning top. Here, \(M = T^*\text{SO}(3)\), where \(\text{SO}(3)\) is the rotation group in \(\mathbb{R}^3\), \(\omega = dp_\varphi \wedge d\varphi + dp_\psi \wedge d\psi + dp_\theta\wedge d\theta\), and

\[ \begin{split} \text{H}(p(t),q(t)) &= \frac{1}{2} \bigg\{ \frac{[(p_\varphi (t) - p_\psi(t) \cos \theta) \sin \psi(t) + p_\theta(t) \sin \theta(t) \cos \psi(t)]^2}{I_1 \sin^2 \theta} \\ &+ \frac{[(p_\varphi(t) - p_\psi (t)\cos \theta(t)) \cos \psi(t) - p_\theta (t)\sin \theta (t) \sin \psi(t)]^2}{I_2 \sin^2 \theta} + \frac{p_\psi^2(t)}{I_3} \bigg\} + mgl \cos \theta(t), \end{split} \]

where \(g \in \mathbb{R}\) is the gravitational acceleration, \(I_i\) (\(i \in \{1,2,3\}\)) are components of the top's moment of inertia tensor, \(m \in \mathbb{R}\) is its mass, and \(l \in \mathbb{R}\) is the magnitude of the vector determining its center of mass at \(t = 0\).

Proposition 1

Given the Darboux coordinates \(\{p^i(t), q_i(t)\}_{i \in [1,n]}\), we can express the Hamiltonian vector field \(X_H\) as:

\[ X_H = \sum_{i \in [1,n]} \left(\frac{\partial H}{\partial p_i} (p(t),q(t)) \frac{\partial}{\partial q^i} - \frac{\partial H}{\partial q^i} (p(t),q(t)) \frac{\partial}{\partial p_i} \right). \]

Let \(X_H\) be as expressed above; we must verify that it satisfies the Hamiltonian condition:

\[ \begin{split} - \iota_{X_H} \omega &= - \sum_{i \in [1,n]} \iota_{X_H} \left( dp^i \wedge dq_i\right) \\ &= - \left[ \sum_{i \in [1,n]} (\iota_{X_H} dp_i) dq^i - \sum_{i \in [1,n]} (\iota_{X_H} q^i) dp_i \right] \\ &= \sum_{i \in [1,n]} \left[\frac{\partial H}{\partial q_i}(p(t),q(t)) \right] dq^i + \sum_{i \in [1,n]} \left[\frac{\partial H}{\partial q^i}(p(t),q(t)) \right]dp_i \\ &= d\text{H}. \end{split} \]
□
